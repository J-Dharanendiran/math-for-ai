# 🔢 The Counting Family: Factorial, Permutations & Combinations

> *"Mathematics is not about numbers, equations, computations, or algorithms: it is about understanding."*
> — William Paul Thurston

---

There is a pattern hiding inside three formulas that most textbooks present as separate ideas. Once you see it, you cannot unsee it. Factorial, permutations, and combinations are not three different tools — they are the **same idea, asked at different depths.**

Let us trace the whole story using just four letters: **A, B, C, D.**

---

## The Root of Everything — Factorial 🌱

Before permutations or combinations exist, there is one fundamental question:

**"How many ways can I arrange ALL of these items?"**

Take A, B, C, D — four distinct letters. You want to line all four of them up. How many different lineups exist?

Think of it as filling four empty slots:

```
[ _ ]  [ _ ]  [ _ ]  [ _ ]
```

- For the **1st slot** — you have **4 choices** (any of A, B, C, D)
- For the **2nd slot** — one is already placed, so **3 choices** remain
- For the **3rd slot** — **2 choices** remain
- For the **4th slot** — only **1 choice** left

So the total arrangements are:

$$4! = 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 24$$

That exclamation mark `!` is called the **factorial symbol.** It simply means: *multiply this number by every positive integer below it until you reach 1.*

A few to remember by heart:

| Expression | Expanded | Result |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| $5!$ | $5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1$ | $120$ |
| $4!$ | $4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1$ | $24$ |
| $3!$ | $3 \times 2 \times 1$ | $6$ |
| $0!$ | *(defined)* | $1$ |

> 💡 **Why is $0! = 1$?** It looks suspicious at first. But mathematically, it is defined as 1 so that the formulas for permutations and combinations remain consistent — especially when you choose *all* items from a set, leaving nothing behind.

Factorial answers: *"Arrange everything."*

That is the **parent idea.** Every formula that follows is built on top of it.

---

## The First Child — Permutation 🔀

Factorial was easy — you used *everything.* But what happens when someone says:

**"From A, B, C, D — arrange only 3 of them."**

Now you are not using all 4. You are selecting a smaller group *and* placing them in order. This is a **permutation.**

The slots look like this:

```
[ _ ]  [ _ ]  [ _ ]
```

- **1st slot:** 4 choices
- **2nd slot:** 3 choices
- **3rd slot:** 2 choices

$$4 \times 3 \times 2 = 24$$

In formula form, this is written as:

$$nPr = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$$

Plugging in $n = 4$, $r = 3$:

$$4P3 = \frac{4!}{(4-3)!} = \frac{4!}{1!} = \frac{24}{1} = 24$$

The denominator $1!$ quietly cancels away the parts of factorial that you did *not* use — the 4th position that was never filled.

**Why does order matter here?**

Consider just two arrangements from those 24:

- **ABC**
- **BAC**

These contain the same three letters. But permutation counts them as *different* because the **sequence changed.** A is in position 1 in the first case, position 2 in the second. In the real world, this is the difference between:

- *A finishes 1st, B finishes 2nd, C finishes 3rd*
- *B finishes 1st, A finishes 2nd, C finishes 3rd*

Those are absolutely not the same result. Order is everything.

> 🎯 **Permutation keyword radar:** Whenever you see words like *arrange, rank, assign positions, create passwords, seat people in order* — that is your signal. Permutation.

---

## The Second Child — Combination 🤝

Now the question shifts:

**"From A, B, C, D — choose only 3 of them. Order does not matter."**

This is a **combination.** You are forming a *group*, not a *lineup.*

Let's list every group of 3 from {A, B, C, D}:

1. **{A, B, C}**
2. **{A, B, D}**
3. **{A, C, D}**
4. **{B, C, D}**

That is 4 groups. The formula confirms it:

$$nCr = \frac{n!}{(n-r)! \cdot r!}$$

$$4C3 = \frac{4!}{(4-3)! \cdot 3!} = \frac{24}{1 \times 6} = 4$$

**But why divide by $r!$?**

This is the most important insight in the whole topic.

Take just one group — **{A, B, C}.** If you were counting permutations, you would count all its arrangements:

```
ABC   ACB   BAC   BCA   CAB   CBA
```

That is $3! = 6$ arrangements — all of the same group.

In a combination, *you do not care about order.* All 6 of those arrangements represent the **same group** of people, same team, same set. So you divide the permutation count by $r!$ to collapse all those duplicates into one.

$$nCr = \frac{nPr}{r!}$$

This is not a trick. It is the literal mathematical act of saying: *"I overcounted by a factor of $r!$, so let me correct that."*

> 🎯 **Combination keyword radar:** Whenever you see *select a team, choose questions, form a committee, pick items from a menu* — that is your signal. Combination.

---

## Seeing the Family Tree 🌳

Now place all three side by side, using the **same example** throughout:

| Question | Formula | Answer |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Arrange **all 4** letters of {A,B,C,D} | $4! = 24$ | **24** |
| Arrange **3 out of 4** letters | $4P3 = \frac{4!}{1!} = 24$ | **24** |
| Choose **3 out of 4** letters (no order) | $4C3 = \frac{4!}{1! \cdot 3!} = 4$ | **4** |

The progression is clean:

$$\text{Factorial} \longrightarrow \text{Permutation} \longrightarrow \text{Combination}$$

- **Factorial** arranges *everything*
- **Permutation** arranges *some things* — still cares about order
- **Combination** selects *some things* — does not care about order

Each step takes the idea further. Each formula inherits from the one before it.

---

## The One Question That Decides Everything 🧭

Before writing any formula, ask yourself this single question:

> **"If I swap two chosen items, does the answer change?"**

- If **yes** → the order matters → use **Permutation**
- If **no** → the order does not matter → use **Combination**

**Example A:** Assigning 1st, 2nd, 3rd place in a race.
Swap two runners → you get a different result. ✅ **Permutation.**

**Example B:** Choosing 3 players for a football team.
Swap two players → it is still the same team. ✅ **Combination.**

This question works in 99% of cases. When in doubt, run it.

---

## When Items Repeat — The Hidden Complexity 🔁

There is one more layer. What if the items are not all distinct?

Consider the word **ALABAMA** — 7 letters, but the letter **A** appears **4 times.**

If all 7 letters were unique, you would have $7! = 5040$ arrangements. But the four A's are identical. Swapping them with each other creates no new arrangement — yet the formula counts it as one.

So you correct for the overcounting by dividing by the factorial of each repeat:

$$\frac{7!}{4!} = \frac{5040}{24} = 210$$

The word **MISSISSIPPI** pushes this further — 11 letters with three sets of repeats:

- **I** appears 4 times → divide by $4!$
- **S** appears 4 times → divide by $4!$
- **P** appears 2 times → divide by $2!$

$$\frac{11!}{4! \times 4! \times 2!} = 34{,}650 \text{ unique arrangements}$$

The general form is:

$$\frac{n!}{a! \cdot b! \cdot c! \cdots}$$

where $a, b, c$ are the counts of each repeated item. It is still the same family — factorial as the base, and division to eliminate what was counted more than once.

---

## 🌀 Deepest Realization

The entire system is built on one act: **counting by multiplication.**

When you fill positions one by one — 4 choices, then 3, then 2 — you are multiplying your way through a decision tree. Factorial captures the full tree. Permutation prunes it to only $r$ levels. Combination then collapses branches that look different but represent the same group.

Every formula is just **factorial with corrections.**

Divide by $(n-r)!$ to stop early.
Divide by $r!$ to remove duplicate groupings.
Divide by $a! \cdot b! \cdots$ to remove duplicate identical items.

The moment you see this, the three formulas stop being things to memorize. They become things to *derive* — any time you forget one, you can rebuild it from the core logic.

---

## ✅ Final Takeaway

| Concept | Core Question | Formula |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| **Factorial** | How many ways to arrange *all* items? | $n!$ |
| **Permutation** | How many ways to arrange *some* items in order? | $\dfrac{n!}{(n-r)!}$ |
| **Combination** | How many ways to *choose* some items (no order)? | $\dfrac{n!}{(n-r)! \cdot r!}$ |
| **Repeated items** | How many unique arrangements when items repeat? | $\dfrac{n!}{a! \cdot b! \cdot c! \cdots}$ |

**Permutation = Position matters.**
**Combination = Choice matters.**

And underneath both of them, quietly doing all the heavy lifting, is factorial — the original idea that counting arrangements means multiplying your choices at every step.
