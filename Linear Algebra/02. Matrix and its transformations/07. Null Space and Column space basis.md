# 🏗️ One Matrix, Two Worlds: Basis, Column Space, and the Null Space Split

> *"The same matrix is secretly telling two stories at the same time — one about what it can reach, and one about what it destroys."*

You've already seen what a column space is. You've already seen what a null space is. Now comes the chapter where those two ideas stop being separate concepts and start being *one coherent picture of the same machine.*

It begins with a specific matrix — four column vectors, three rows, and a question that seems routine until it isn't.

---

## The Setup: Four Vectors, One Matrix

Take the $3 \times 4$ matrix $A$ built from these columns:

$$\mathbf{v_1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v_2} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v_3} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 4 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v_4} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

The column space is, by definition, $\text{Span}\{v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4\}$. But that statement alone answers nothing useful. It doesn't tell you whether these four vectors are actually pulling in independent directions, or whether some of them are redundant passengers — pointing somewhere the others already cover.

And before you can name a *basis* for the column space, you need to know which vectors actually earn their place.

---

## What a Basis Actually Is

You asked for the reminder, so here it is — stated precisely, not vaguely.

A **basis** for a subspace is a set of vectors that does two things simultaneously:

1. **Spans the subspace** — every vector in the subspace can be built as a linear combination of the basis vectors.
2. **Is linearly independent** — no vector in the set is redundant; none can be built from the others.

A basis is the *smallest* set that still generates everything. Think of it as the exact skeleton of a subspace — nothing missing, nothing wasted.

A plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$ has infinitely many vectors in it. But its basis has exactly two — two independent directions that together reach every point on that plane. The plane is 2-dimensional because its basis has two elements. The dimension of a subspace is always the size of its basis.

---

## Step 1: Row Reduce to Find the Truth

To find which columns are independent, we solve $Ax = \vec{0}$ by row-reducing $A$ to RREF. The null space of a matrix and the null space of its RREF are identical — so the row operations don't destroy the information we need.

The row reduction proceeds:

- Keep Row 1 as $(1, 1, 1, 1)$.
- New Row 2: $2R_1 - R_2 \rightarrow (0, 1, -2, -1)$.
- New Row 3: $R_3 - 3R_1 \rightarrow (0, 1, -2, -1)$.
- Zero out above the second pivot: Row 1 becomes $(1, 0, 3, 2)$.
- Row 3 minus Row 2: identical rows cancel, leaving $(0, 0, 0, 0)$.

The RREF is:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 3 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 & -2 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Two pivot columns: columns 1 and 2. Two columns without pivots: columns 3 and 4.

---

## Step 2: Reading the Null Space

Translating the RREF back into equations:

$$x_1 + 3x_3 + 2x_4 = 0 \implies x_1 = -3x_3 - 2x_4$$
$$x_2 - 2x_3 - x_4 = 0 \implies x_2 = 2x_3 + x_4$$

Here, $x_3$ and $x_4$ are **free variables** — you can set them to anything. Once you do, $x_1$ and $x_2$ are forced. The entire null space is:

$$x = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_4 \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The null space is not just the zero vector. It's a 2-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^4$ — a whole plane of inputs that the matrix sends to zero. This immediately tells you the four column vectors are **linearly dependent**: if the only solution to $Ax = \vec{0}$ were $x = \vec{0}$, the columns would be independent. They aren't.

---

## Step 3: The Confusion You Caught

Then came the sharpest moment of the session. You'd seen that pivot variables ($x_1, x_2$) depend on the free variables ($x_3, x_4$). And you asked:

> *"But we declined pivot elements when it comes to linear independence?"*

This is the confusion that gets almost everyone. Let's separate it cleanly.

There are **two entirely different objects** in play here:

**In the null space**, you're studying the *coefficients* $x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4$. The free variables $x_3, x_4$ are the independent choices — you set them freely, and the pivot variables $x_1, x_2$ follow. So the null space basis comes from the free-variable directions.

**In the column space**, you're studying the *columns themselves* $v_1, v_2, v_3, v_4$. The pivot *columns* (columns 1 and 2) are the independent ones — they point in genuinely new directions. The free-variable columns (columns 3 and 4) can be built from them.

| View | What you're studying | What's independent |
|---|---|---|
| Column Space | The column vectors $v_i$ | **Pivot columns** $v_1, v_2$ |
| Null Space | The coefficients $x_i$ | **Free variables** $x_3, x_4$ |

Different questions. Different objects. Different answers.

The corrected statement:

> ❌ *"Pivot elements won't add directions anymore and free variables contribute directions."*
>
> ✅ **Pivot columns contribute the independent directions of the column space. Free variables contribute the independent directions of the null space.**

Think of it as a factory. The **pivot columns are the beams** — the structural members holding the shape. The **free variables are the dials** — the knobs you turn to explore every possible blueprint. The beams determine the shape of what the factory builds. The dials determine how many different ways you can describe what it destroys.

---

## Step 4: Using the Null Space to Eliminate Redundancy

Now the null space becomes a tool, not just a result. It tells us exactly *how* $v_3$ and $v_4$ are built from $v_1$ and $v_2$.

**Eliminating $v_4$:** Set $x_3 = 0$, $x_4 = -1$. The equations give $x_1 = 2$, $x_2 = -1$. So:

$$2v_1 - v_2 + 0 \cdot v_3 + (-1)v_4 = \vec{0} \implies v_4 = 2v_1 - v_2$$

$v_4$ is completely constructible from $v_1$ and $v_2$. It's redundant.

**Eliminating $v_3$:** Set $x_4 = 0$, $x_3 = -1$. The equations give $x_1 = 3$, $x_2 = -2$. So:

$$3v_1 - 2v_2 + (-1)v_3 + 0 \cdot v_4 = \vec{0} \implies v_3 = 3v_1 - 2v_2$$

$v_3$ is also completely constructible from $v_1$ and $v_2$. Also redundant.

Strip them away. What's left is $\{v_1, v_2\}$ — the pivot columns.

---

## Step 5: Confirming the Basis

Two vectors remain. Are they independent? The check for two vectors is simple: one would have to be a scalar multiple of the other.

- Matching the first entry of $v_2$ to $v_1$ requires scalar $c = 1$, giving $(1, 2, 3)^T \neq (1, 1, 4)^T$.
- Matching the second entry requires $c = 1/2$, giving $(0.5, 1, 1.5)^T \neq (1, 1, 4)^T$.
- Matching the third requires $c = 4/3$.

No single scalar works. The vectors point in genuinely different directions.

Therefore, the **basis for $\text{Col}(A)$** is:

$$\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \right\}$$

Geometrically: a plane inside $\mathbb{R}^3$. Every output this matrix can possibly produce lives on that plane. Every vector outside it is unreachable.

---

## The Two Stories, Side by Side

This is the moment where the matrix stops being a grid of numbers and becomes a complete picture.

The matrix $A$ takes inputs from $\mathbb{R}^4$ and produces outputs in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Those are different spaces. And both the column space and the null space live in their own respective worlds:

| | Column Space | Null Space |
|---|---|---|
| **Question** | What outputs are reachable? | Which inputs are destroyed? |
| **Lives in** | $\mathbb{R}^3$ (output world) | $\mathbb{R}^4$ (input world) |
| **Built from** | Pivot columns | Free variables |
| **Dimension** | 2 (rank) | 2 (nullity) |
| **Geometric shape** | A plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$ | A plane in $\mathbb{R}^4$ |

Notice: they don't even live in the same universe. That's why comparing them directly confuses people — you're looking at objects from different spaces and expecting them to rhyme. They don't need to. They describe opposite ends of the same machine.

---

## The Rank-Nullity Split

For this matrix: rank = 2, nullity = 2. And $2 + 2 = 4$ — the number of columns, the dimension of the input space.

This isn't a coincidence. The input space $\mathbb{R}^4$ splits cleanly into two parts:

```
Input Space ℝ⁴
┌──────────────────────┐
│   Null Space         │  ← 2 directions destroyed
│   (2 dimensions)     │
└──────────────────────┘
┌──────────────────────┐
│   Useful Inputs      │  ← 2 directions survive
│   (2 dimensions)     │
└──────────────────────┘
```

The 2 directions the matrix *kills* make up the null space. The 2 directions that *survive* contribute to the 2-dimensional column space.

Every input direction either gets destroyed or gets through. The total must always add up to $n$:

$$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$$

This is the **Rank-Nullity Theorem** — and what it's really saying is that a matrix cannot both destroy many directions *and* reach far into the output space. The more it forgets, the more constrained its reach. Information lost in the input means reach lost in the output.

---

## Sanity Check

- **Why do we use the null space to find the column space basis?** Because the null space tells us which columns are dependent — and dependent columns are the ones to remove. The free-variable vectors encode the dependency relationships directly.
- **If $v_3 = 3v_1 - 2v_2$, why is $v_3$ redundant?** Because any output you'd produce using $v_3$ can already be produced using $v_1$ and $v_2$ with coefficients adjusted accordingly. It adds no new direction.
- **Can the null space basis and the column space basis share vectors?** No — they live in different spaces ($\mathbb{R}^4$ vs $\mathbb{R}^3$). You can't even meaningfully compare them.
- **What does it mean if the null space contains only $\vec{0}$?** The columns are linearly independent. The matrix is injective — it never maps two different inputs to the same output. Nullity = 0, rank = $n$.

---

## 🌉 ML Bridge

Both of these spaces matter in practice, and understanding them together is what separates someone who knows the formulas from someone who understands the system.

**Multicollinearity** in a dataset is exactly the scenario above: some features ($v_3$, $v_4$) can be written as linear combinations of others ($v_1$, $v_2$). The feature matrix has a non-trivial null space. When you try to fit a linear model, the system has infinitely many solutions — any combination along the null space direction produces identical predictions. Regularization techniques like Ridge regression are directly combating this by shrinking along null-space directions.

**Dimensionality reduction** via PCA is essentially finding the column space basis of the data matrix — identifying the pivot directions that actually carry independent variance, and discarding the rest. The null space of the data covariance matrix corresponds to directions with no variance — pure redundancy.

**Neural network weight matrices** live in this framework too. A layer with more inputs than outputs necessarily has a non-trivial null space: some input directions are completely ignored. Understanding which inputs the network "hears" and which it discards is the geometry of the null space, applied at scale.

---

## 🌀 Deepest Realization

Before this session, the null space and the column space probably felt like two separate homework topics. Now they're the same story from opposite ends.

The **null space** is the set of inputs the matrix cannot distinguish from zero. The **column space** is the set of outputs the matrix can actually reach.

One lives in the input world. One lives in the output world. But they're connected by a conservation law: every dimension the matrix destroys in the null space is a dimension it cannot contribute to the column space. The total is always $n$.

And here's the even deeper version: the *basis* of the column space comes from pivot columns, which are exactly the columns that are *not* in any null space relationship. The null space is built from free variables, which are exactly the columns that *are* redundant. The pivot/free split is doing two jobs at once — identifying the independent directions for output, and identifying the dependent directions for the null space.

> **One row reduction. Two complete pictures of the machine.**

RREF doesn't just simplify a system. It simultaneously reveals what the matrix can reach and what it destroys. That's why it's the central algorithm of linear algebra.

---

## ✅ Final Takeaway

A $3 \times 4$ matrix $A$ with rank 2 tells you everything through its RREF:

- **Pivot columns** ($v_1$, $v_2$) are linearly independent → they form the **basis for the column space**.
- **Free-variable columns** ($v_3$, $v_4$) are dependent → they generate the **basis for the null space**.
- The column space is a **plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$** — all reachable outputs.
- The null space is a **plane in $\mathbb{R}^4$** — all destroyed inputs.
- Rank (2) + Nullity (2) = 4 = number of columns. Always.

The basis isn't just "some vectors that span the space." It's the *minimal* set — nothing redundant, nothing missing. Finding it means using the null space to expose the redundant columns, then keeping only the pivot columns that genuinely contribute new directions.

One matrix. Two worlds. One clean picture. 🧠
