# 🌐 Span — How Far Can Vectors Take You?

---

> *"A single arrow points somewhere. The question is not where it points — but how much of the world you can build if you're allowed to scale it, reverse it, and combine it with others."*

---

The previous chapter ended with a line.

You built it. You walked it. You proved it moves through any dimension — 2D, 3D, even the 512-dimensional embedding spaces that language models live in — using nothing more than a point, a direction, and a parameter $t$.

But a line is just *one direction*. And one direction, no matter how far you walk it, only ever gives you a line.

So a question naturally follows:

*What if you had more than one direction? How much of space could you reach then?*

That question is not a sidebar. It is the entire chapter. And the concept that answers it — **span** — is quietly one of the most important ideas in all of machine learning.

---

## The Limits of One Direction 🧱

Start small. Suppose you have one vector:

$$\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the unit vector $\hat{i}$ — pointing one step right.

Now, you're allowed to scale it. Any real number $c$ is fair game:

$$c \cdot \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} c \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Scale it forward: $(2, 0)$, $(5, 0)$, $(100, 0)$.  
Scale it backward: $(-1, 0)$, $(-3, 0)$, $(-\infty, 0)$.  
Scale it to nothing: $(0, 0)$.

You can reach every point of the form $(c,\ 0)$. Every single one. An infinite number of points.

But they all live on the same **horizontal line**.

**The $y$-coordinate is always zero. You never leave the $x$-axis.** No matter how cleverly you scale $\vec{v}$, no matter how large or small you make $c$, you cannot reach $(0, 1)$. You cannot reach $(3, 7)$. The entire vertical direction is completely invisible to you.

One vector gives you one direction of freedom. That freedom traces out exactly one line through the origin.

That line — that full set of reachable points — is called the **span** of $\vec{v}$.

$$\text{span}(\vec{v}) = \{c\vec{v} \mid c \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

*All the points you can build. Nothing more, nothing less.*

Think of it like a game where your character can only move left and right. No matter how long you play, you cannot reach anything above or below the horizontal. Your reachable world is a line — not because your steps are too small, but because ***you only have one direction of movement***.

---

## The Linear Combination — The Construction Rule 🔧

Before adding a second vector, name the operation cleanly. It will come up in every chapter that follows.

A **linear combination** of vectors $\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_n$ is any expression of the form:

$$c_1\vec{v}_1 + c_2\vec{v}_2 + \cdots + c_n\vec{v}_n$$

where $c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n$ are real numbers called **scalars** — they scale each vector before the addition.

> *"A linear combination is a recipe. The vectors are your ingredients. The scalars tell you how much of each to use."*

No restrictions on the scalars. No limits on the values. You can stretch, shrink, reverse, and mix however you like.

The **span** of a set of vectors is then the collection of *all possible* linear combinations of those vectors:

$$\text{span}(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2, \ldots, \vec{v}_n) = \{c_1\vec{v}_1 + c_2\vec{v}_2 + \cdots + c_n\vec{v}_n \mid c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_n \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

**Span is not about one combination. It is the exhaustive catalogue of every combination possible.**

In other words: span is the complete set of all vectors that *can be represented* using the given vectors. Every vector reachable by scaling and adding. Every point your ingredients are capable of producing.

That framing matters. Span is not a process — it is a result. It is the finished structure after you've run every possible recipe.

---

## A Second Vector Changes Everything 🔓

Now add a second vector:

$$\vec{w} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is $\hat{j}$ — pointing one step up.

You are now allowed to combine any scalar multiple of $\vec{v}$ with any scalar multiple of $\vec{w}$:

$$c_1 \vec{v} + c_2 \vec{w} = c_1\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

And here, something dramatic happens.

$c_1$ is a free real number. So is $c_2$. *Independently.* Together, they can produce **any pair of real numbers**. Any point $(c_1, c_2)$ in the entire plane is reachable.

| Choose | Get |
|---|---|
| $c_1 = 3,\ c_2 = 4$ | $(3, 4)$ |
| $c_1 = -2,\ c_2 = 7$ | $(-2, 7)$ |
| $c_1 = 0,\ c_2 = 0$ | $(0, 0)$ |
| $c_1 = \pi,\ c_2 = \sqrt{2}$ | $(\pi, \sqrt{2})$ |

*Every point. Every single point in $\mathbb{R}^2$.*

$$\text{span}(\vec{v}, \vec{w}) = \mathbb{R}^2$$

**One vector filled a line. Two independent vectors filled an entire plane.**

That explosive difference — from a line to all of $\mathbb{R}^2$ — is not arithmetic. It is geometry. And it is the exact idea that span is built to capture.

Back to the game analogy: now your character can move left-right *and* up-down. Two independent movement abilities. The entire 2D map is suddenly reachable. The world didn't get *slightly* bigger — it jumped from a thread to a plane.

---

## The Trap: Two Vectors, Still Just a Line 🪤

Here is where intuition misleads almost everyone on first contact.

Suppose you have:

$$\vec{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}2\\2\end{bmatrix}$$

Two vectors. You might expect a plane — after all, there are two of them.

Look closely. $\vec{v}_2 = 2\vec{v}_1$.

$\vec{v}_2$ is $\vec{v}_1$ scaled by $2$. They point in *exactly the same direction* — along the diagonal through the origin. Adding $\vec{v}_2$ to your toolkit gave you nothing new. Every combination you can build with both vectors:

$$c_1(1, 1) + c_2(2, 2) = (c_1 + 2c_2,\ c_1 + 2c_2)$$

always produces a point of the form $(k, k)$ — something where both coordinates are equal. That is one diagonal line, not a plane.

$$\text{span}(\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2) = \text{span}(\vec{v}_1) = \text{a single diagonal line}$$

> *"A vector that is secretly a scaled copy of another vector is not a new direction. It is the same direction wearing a disguise."*

In the game analogy: suppose your character had two movement abilities — *move diagonally northeast* and *move diagonally northeast, but twice as fast*. Both abilities look different. But they go the same direction. Your reachable world is still just a diagonal line. **Fake freedom. The numbers changed. The direction didn't.**

This is called **linear dependence** — the technical name for the situation where one vector in a set can already be expressed as a combination of the others. Dependent vectors are redundant. They inflate the count without expanding the span.

The opposite — where no vector in the set can be built from the others — is called **linear independence**. Independent vectors each contribute a genuinely new direction. *Only independent vectors expand the span.*

The actual deciding factor in span is not the count of vectors. It is the count of **independent directions**.

---

## The Geometry: Three Possible Worlds 🗺️

The span of a set of vectors always collapses into a recognizable geometric shape. Which shape depends entirely on how many independent directions your vectors actually provide.

**World 1 — The Line**

One vector. Or multiple vectors that are all scaled copies of each other.

Movement is possible only forward and backward along one axis. A single thread through the origin.

$$\text{span}\left(\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}\right) = \text{a line through the origin in direction } (2, 1)$$

**World 2 — The Plane**

Two vectors pointing in genuinely different directions — neither a scaled copy of the other.

Movement is possible in two independent axes. The entire 2D plane opens up.

$$\text{span}\left(\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}\right) = \mathbb{R}^2$$

**World 3 — Full Space**

Three vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$, each pointing in a direction the others cannot replicate.

Movement is possible in all three axes — left-right, forward-backward, up-down. Every point in 3D space is reachable.

$$\text{span}\left(\hat{i},\ \hat{j},\ \hat{k}\right) = \mathbb{R}^3$$

The pattern, written cleanly:

| Independent directions | Span |
|---|---|
| 1 | A line through the origin |
| 2 in $\mathbb{R}^2$ | All of $\mathbb{R}^2$ |
| 2 in $\mathbb{R}^3$ | A plane through the origin |
| 3 in $\mathbb{R}^3$ | All of $\mathbb{R}^3$ |

**Span is about how many independent directions you control — not how many vectors you have.**

## Span as a Question 🤔

A useful operational lens: span answers a very specific question about a target vector.

> *"Can this target vector be reached using these building blocks?"*

Formally — is a vector $\vec{b}$ in the span of $\{\vec{v}_1, \vec{v}_2\}$?

This is equivalent to asking: do there exist scalars $c_1, c_2$ such that:

$$c_1\vec{v}_1 + c_2\vec{v}_2 = \vec{b}$$

- If yes — $\vec{b}$ is in the span. It is reachable.  
- If no — $\vec{b}$ is outside the span. It is inaccessible with these building blocks.

Example. Is $\vec{b} = (3, 3)$ in the span of $\vec{v}_1 = (1, 0)$ and $\vec{v}_2 = (0, 1)$?

$$c_1(1, 0) + c_2(0, 1) = (3, 3)$$
$$\Rightarrow c_1 = 3,\ c_2 = 3 \quad \checkmark$$

Yes. Reachable.

Now ask the same question with only $\vec{v}_1 = (1, 0)$.

$$c_1(1, 0) = (3, 3)$$
$$\Rightarrow (c_1, 0) = (3, 3)$$

That requires $0 = 3$. Impossible. $(3, 3)$ cannot be reached from a single horizontal vector — the vertical component has nowhere to come from.

**Checking membership in a span is the same as asking whether a system of equations has a solution.** This is not a coincidence — it is the bridge between geometry and algebra that all of linear algebra is built on.

---

## Why "Through the Origin"? 🎯

You might have noticed: every span mentioned above passes through the origin. The line passes through $(0, 0)$. The plane contains $(0, 0)$. This is always true, and for a simple reason.

When all scalars are set to zero:

$$0 \cdot \vec{v}_1 + 0 \cdot \vec{v}_2 + \cdots + 0 \cdot \vec{v}_n = \vec{0}$$

The zero vector is always reachable. The origin is always included.

This is not a technicality to memorize — it is a *structural constraint* that defines an important class of objects in linear algebra called **subspaces**: spaces that always contain the origin and are closed under scaling and addition. The span of any set of vectors is always a subspace.

*Lines, planes, and full spaces are all subspaces — as long as they contain the origin.*

A plane that does *not* pass through the origin — like the floor of a room — is **not** a subspace, even though it looks like a plane. That may seem like an arbitrary rule now. By the time you reach matrices and null spaces, it will feel inevitable.

## The ML Bridge 🤖

Span is not mathematical decoration. It sits at the functional core of how machine learning systems work — and understanding it changes how you *read* what these systems are actually doing.

**Embeddings live in a spanned space.**

When a language model encodes the word *"king"* as a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{512}$, that vector is a point in a high-dimensional space. The meaningful directions in that space — the directions that encode concepts like royalty, gender, age, geography — are the *effective spanning set* of the embedding space. The model learns which directions matter. The rest of the space is unused. Span tells you what a set of learned vectors is actually *covering*.

**The expressiveness of a neural network layer is a span question.**

A linear layer computes $W\vec{x}$, where $W$ is a matrix. The *columns* of $W$ are vectors. The span of those columns — called the **column space** of $W$ — is exactly the set of outputs the layer can ever produce, regardless of what input $\vec{x}$ you give it.

If $W$ is a $512 \times 512$ matrix but its columns all point in the same 10-dimensional subspace, then no matter what input you feed it, the output is always confined to that 10-dimensional space. The layer has 512 columns but only 10 independent directions. Its span is small. Its expressiveness is bottlenecked.

> *"A neural network layer cannot output what its column space does not contain. Span is the hard ceiling on what a layer can express."*

This is measured by a quantity called **rank** — the number of independent directions in the span of the matrix's columns. A rank-deficient matrix has partially redundant columns: some directions are linearly dependent, the span is smaller than it appears, the layer's capacity is quietly wasted.

**PCA finds the span that matters.**

Principal Component Analysis takes high-dimensional data and asks: *what is the smallest subspace — the smallest span — that contains most of the variation in this dataset?*

It finds the most efficient spanning set for the data's significant directions. When you reduce 1000-dimensional data to 10 principal components, you are replacing the full span of $\mathbb{R}^{1000}$ with a 10-dimensional subspace that the data actually lives near. The math underneath is entirely about which directions are independent and which are redundant.

**Attention mechanisms construct linear combinations.**

When a transformer's attention head attends to certain tokens, it is constructing a linear combination of those token embeddings — a weighted sum of value vectors, with the attention weights as the scalars.

What that attention head can express is bounded by the span of the value vectors it has access to. If two tokens have nearly identical value vectors, they are linearly dependent — attending to one versus the other changes nothing meaningful in the output. Redundancy in the attention pattern is linear dependence in the value span.

## The Whole Picture 🌐

Start with the simplest possible object: a vector. Scale it. You get a line. That is the span of one vector.

Add another vector — a genuinely independent direction. Combine any multiples of both. Suddenly you can reach an entire plane. The span doubled the dimension.

Add a third independent direction in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Reach all of three-dimensional space.

Add redundant vectors — ones secretly pointing in a direction you already have. The span does not grow. The dimension stays flat. The world does not expand.

**Span measures not how many vectors you have, but how much of space they collectively generate.**

This is the reason the previous chapter built toward this one. A parametric line — $\vec{a} + t\vec{v}$ — is motion in one direction. One direction in the span. But the moment you have a second independent direction, the world jumps from a thread to a plane. The geometry changes *qualitatively*, not just quantitatively.

> *"Vectors are the building blocks. Linear combinations are the construction rule. Span is the finished structure."*

And this structure — this vocabulary of spans, subspaces, independent directions — is not a prerequisite you clear before getting to the real thing.

***It is the real thing.***

Every matrix, every transformation, every neural network, every dimensionality reduction, every attention mechanism is ultimately a story about which directions are independent, what subspace they span, and whether the vector you care about lives inside or outside that span.

---

*Next: if span tells you the space you can build, what's the most efficient way to build it? Some collections of vectors are wasteful — they repeat directions they already have. The leanest possible spanning set, the one with no redundancy, no dependence, no dead weight — that is called a **basis**. And a basis gives a space its coordinate system.* 📐
