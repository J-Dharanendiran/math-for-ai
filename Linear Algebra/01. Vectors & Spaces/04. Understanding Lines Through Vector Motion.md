# 🧭 Lines Are Not Equations. They Are Motion.

---

> *"A line is not a relationship between x and y. It is a point that decided to keep moving."*

---

The previous chapter ended with a quiet revelation: every vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is built from two primitive directions — $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$ — scaled and added together. Every vector is a **recipe**, and unit vectors are its ingredients.

That idea changed how you *read* vectors. This chapter changes how you *use* them.

Because once you understand that vectors carry direction, a natural question surfaces:

*What if you don't just place a vector — what if you let it move?*

That question is the entire chapter. And the answer rewrites what a line actually is.

---

## What School Taught You (And What It Was Hiding)

You've seen lines written like this:

$$y = 2x + 1$$

That notation is called **slope-intercept form**, and for most of school mathematics, it's perfectly fine. It tells you the line has slope $2$ and crosses the $y$-axis at $1$. Draw the graph. Move on.

But the formula is quietly making a very specific assumption: that a line is a *relationship between $x$ and $y$*. The equation is describing the line **from the outside** — telling you how $y$ depends on $x$, as if the line were just a rule you're observing.

Linear algebra does something different.

It builds the line **from the inside**.

Instead of describing what the line looks like, it *generates* the line — point by point — using a vector and a parameter. And that shift, from *describing* to *generating*, is the conceptual leap this entire chapter is about.

> *"Slope-intercept form watches the line from a distance. The vector form walks it."*

---

## One Direction. Infinite Points.

Start simple. Suppose you have a vector:

$$\vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Its tip lands at $(2, 1)$. That's one point.

Now scale it by a real number $t$:

$$t\vec{v} = t\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2t \\ t \end{bmatrix}$$

Watch what $t$ does:

| $t$ | Point generated |
|---|---|
| $0$ | $(0, 0)$ |
| $1$ | $(2, 1)$ |
| $2$ | $(4, 2)$ |
| $-1$ | $(-2, -1)$ |
| $0.5$ | $(1, 0.5)$ |

Each value of $t$ produces a different point. And every single one of those points lies on the **same straight line**.

This is not a coincidence. It is the definition. As $t$ sweeps through every real number — positive, negative, fractional, enormous — the expression $t\vec{v}$ traces out infinitely many points. All in a line. All through the origin.

**A line is not drawn. It is generated.**

And it always passes through the origin here because when $t = 0$, you get $\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$ — no matter what $\vec{v}$ is. The origin is always included.

That's the first form:

$$\ell = \{t\vec{v} \mid t \in \mathbb{R}\}$$

All possible scalar multiples of a single vector. One direction. Infinite points. One line.

---

## Shifting the Line Off the Origin

Now suppose you want a *parallel* line — same direction, but passing through a different point entirely. The expression $t\vec{v}$ won't help here. It's anchored to the origin.

The fix is simple: **add a position vector**.

Let $\vec{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$ be a point you want the line to pass through. Define:

$$\vec{x} + t\vec{v}$$

This expression now means something geometrically precise:

- $\vec{x}$ is where you **start**
- $\vec{v}$ is the **direction** you travel
- $t$ is **how far** you go

When $t = 0$, you sit at $\vec{x}$. As $t$ increases, you move in the direction of $\vec{v}$. As $t$ decreases, you move the other way.

The line is the set of all positions you can reach by varying $t$. You're not drawing it — you're *walking* it.

This is called a **parametric line**, and $t$ is the **parameter** — the variable that controls motion along the line.

> *"$t$ is not a coordinate. It is a timestamp. It tells you where along the journey you are."*

The two pieces — position and direction — together specify any line in any space. Not just $\mathbb{R}^2$. *Any space.*

---

## Building a Line Between Two Points

Here is where the real machinery clicks in. Suppose you're given two points and asked to find the line through both of them.

$$\vec{a} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \vec{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

The first thing you need is a **direction**. And the direction from $\vec{a}$ to $\vec{b}$ is exactly their difference:

$$\vec{b} - \vec{a} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 - 2 \\ 3 - 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

*Subtraction between points gives displacement. That is a fundamental idea in both geometry and physics.*

You now have everything:

- **A point on the line**: $\vec{b} = (0, 3)$
- **The direction of the line**: $\vec{b} - \vec{a} = (-2, 2)$

Plug them into the parametric form:

$$\vec{b} + t(\vec{b} - \vec{a}) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Check the special values:

- At $t = 0$: you land at $\vec{b} = (0, 3)$ ✓
- At $t = -1$: you get $(0, 3) + (-1)(-2, 2) = (0 + 2,\ 3 - 2) = (2, 1) = \vec{a}$ ✓
- At $t > 0$: you travel *past* $\vec{b}$, extending the line beyond it
- At $t < -1$: you travel *past* $\vec{a}$, extending the line the other way

The line is infinite. The two original points are just two particular moments on it — two values of $t$ among infinitely many.

---

## Splitting Into Parametric Equations

The vector equation can be unpacked into individual coordinate equations. Take the same example:

$$(0, 3) + t(-2, 2)$$

Separate by component:

$$x = 0 + t(-2) = -2t$$
$$y = 3 + t(2) = 3 + 2t$$

These are the **parametric equations** of the line.

Notice what they tell you:

- Both $x$ and $y$ depend on $t$ — neither depends on the other
- As $t$ increases by 1, $x$ decreases by 2 and $y$ increases by 2 simultaneously
- The parameter $t$ is the engine driving both coordinates at once

This is a fundamentally different structure from $y = f(x)$. Here, neither variable controls the other. **Both are controlled by a third variable, $t$, running in the background.**

> *"In parametric form, the line moves through time. In slope-intercept form, it sits still and gets described."*

---

## Why Slope Breaks (And Vectors Don't)

The slope $m$ in $y = mx + b$ is doing something secretly familiar — it's encoding how much you move vertically per unit of horizontal movement.

Slope $m = 2$ means: move right 1, move up 2.

Now look at the direction vector from the previous example: $(-2, 2)$.

That vector says: move left 2, move up 2.

*Slope and direction vectors are describing the same thing.* Slope is just a compressed, one-number version of a direction vector — the ratio of the vertical component to the horizontal component.

But compression comes with costs:

1. **Vertical lines are undefined.** The direction vector $(0, 1)$ describes a vertical line effortlessly. Slope? Division by zero. A description method that crashes for one of the most natural lines imaginable.

2. **Three dimensions have no single slope.** A line in $\mathbb{R}^3$ moves in $x$, $y$, *and* $z$ simultaneously. How do you reduce that to one number? You don't. But a direction vector handles it naturally:

$$P(t) = P_1 + t(P_1 - P_2)$$

works identically whether you're in 2D, 3D, or 10,000D.

3. **Slope describes from outside. Vectors generate from inside.** Slope tells you the *appearance* of the line — how steep it looks. The vector form tells you the *construction* of the line — how it's built from a point and a direction.

| | Slope-Intercept Form | Parametric Vector Form |
|---|---|---|
| **Works in 2D?** | ✓ | ✓ |
| **Works in 3D?** | ✗ | ✓ |
| **Works in $\mathbb{R}^n$?** | ✗ | ✓ |
| **Handles vertical lines?** | ✗ | ✓ |
| **Reveals construction?** | ✗ | ✓ |

The slope-intercept form is a *special case*. The vector form is the *general principle*.

---

## A Line in Three Dimensions

To make this concrete, here's a line in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — a space you cannot fully draw, but can absolutely navigate with vectors.

Points:

$$P_1 = (-1, 2, 7) \qquad P_2 = (0, 3, 4)$$

Direction:

$$P_1 - P_2 = (-1 - 0,\ 2 - 3,\ 7 - 4) = (-1, -1, 3)$$

Parametric line:

$$P(t) = P_1 + t(P_1 - P_2) = (-1, 2, 7) + t(-1, -1, 3)$$

Separated by component:

$$x = -1 - t$$
$$y = 2 - t$$
$$z = 7 + 3t$$

As $t$ increases:
- $x$ decreases steadily
- $y$ decreases steadily
- $z$ increases *three times faster*

The line is moving through three-dimensional space. No "slope" captures that. But the vector form handles it in one line of notation — the same notation as in 2D, unchanged.

*That's not a coincidence. It's the point.*

## The ML Bridge 🤖

The parametric line is not just a geometry trick. It is the **literal architecture** of how machine learning models navigate their worlds.

**Gradient descent** — the algorithm that trains every neural network — is a parametric line in disguise. At each step, the model is at a point $\vec{\theta}$ in a high-dimensional parameter space. The gradient gives a *direction*. The learning rate gives a *scalar*. The update step is:

$$\vec{\theta}_{\text{new}} = \vec{\theta} - \alpha \cdot \nabla L(\vec{\theta})$$

This is $\vec{x} + t\vec{v}$. Point. Direction. Scalar. One step along a parametric line through parameter space. Repeat until loss converges.

**Word embeddings** place words as vectors in a space like $\mathbb{R}^{300}$. When you ask a model to find words "similar to king but more like woman than man," it is *literally performing vector subtraction and addition* — finding directions, displacing along them — exactly the arithmetic of $\vec{b} - \vec{a}$ you did above.

**Principal Component Analysis** (PCA) finds the *directions* in a dataset along which the data varies most. Each principal component is a direction vector. Projecting data onto it is sliding every data point along a parametric line — measuring how far each point lands at each $t$.

**Linear layers in neural networks** — the operation $W\vec{x} + \vec{b}$ — transform input vectors using weights $W$ and shift them by bias $\vec{b}$. That "+$\vec{b}$" is the same shift you saw when moving a line off the origin. A bias term in a neural network and a position vector in parametric geometry are the same mathematical object.

> *"When a neural network takes a step, adjusts a weight, or shifts an activation — it is walking a parametric line through a space you cannot visualise. The math is identical to what you just learned."*

The difference between $\vec{a} + t(\vec{b} - \vec{a})$ on paper and gradient descent on a billion-parameter model is not the mathematics. It is the dimensionality.

---

## The Whole Picture

What started as a dissatisfaction with $y = mx + b$ became something much deeper.

1. **A line through the origin** is all scalar multiples of a direction vector: $\{t\vec{v} \mid t \in \mathbb{R}\}$
2. **A line anywhere** is a point plus scaled direction: $\vec{a} + t\vec{v}$
3. **A line through two points** uses their difference as the direction: $\vec{a} + t(\vec{b} - \vec{a})$
4. **Parametric equations** split the vector form into individual coordinates controlled by $t$
5. **Slope is just a compressed direction vector** — a 2D special case with hard limitations
6. **The same formula works in any dimension** — 2D, 3D, or the 512-dimensional embedding space of a language model

None of these are separate facts. They are one continuous idea:

***A line is motion. A point plus a direction plus a parameter. That is all.***

Slope-intercept form describes what the line looks like. The parametric form describes how the line *is made*.

And knowing how something is made is always more powerful than knowing what it looks like.
