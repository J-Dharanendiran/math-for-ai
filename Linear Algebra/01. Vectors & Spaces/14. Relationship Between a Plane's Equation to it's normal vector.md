# 🌀 Relationship Between a Plane's Equation to it's normal vector

---

> *"Mathematics occasionally rewards suffering with elegance. Rare event — but it happens."*

---

You are staring at this expression:

$$\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$$

And your first instinct is correct — *this looks terrifying.* Two cross products, nested, tangled, threatening to eat your afternoon. You expect some wild, twisted vector pointing in a direction no one agreed on. You set up your matrix. You begin expanding. The terms multiply. The subscripts blur. You question your choices.

Then someone hands you Lagrange's formula and says:

> *"Relax. It's just this:"*

$$\boxed{\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})}$$

The terror collapses into **two dot products and a subtraction.** But if you only memorize the formula without understanding *why* — you'll forget it the moment an exam changes the letter arrangement. So let's follow the story all the way through.

---

## 🎯 The First Clue: Where Does the Answer Live?

Before any algebra, ask a geometric question: *where does* $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$ *point?*

Start inside the parentheses. The cross product $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$ creates a vector **perpendicular to both b and c** — it shoots straight out of the plane that b and c define. Call it the *normal* of that plane.

Now you cross **a** with that normal. The cross product of any vector with a normal to a plane must be perpendicular to that normal — which means it **falls back into the plane.**

So the answer to $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$ lives inside the plane of **b** and **c**. Always.

- 📌 It cannot point outside that plane.
- 📌 It cannot have a component along $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$.
- 📌 It must be some combination of **b** and **c** themselves.

Which means the answer *has* to look like:

$$\alpha\,\mathbf{b} + \beta\,\mathbf{c}$$

for some scalars $\alpha$ and $\beta$. The formula is telling us what those scalars are. **That's the whole game.**

---

## 🔬 The Algebra: Where the Scalars Come From

Knowing the answer lives in the plane of **b** and **c** is beautiful geometry — but to find the exact scalars, we have to get our hands dirty.

Write everything in components:

$$\mathbf{a} = (a_x, a_y, a_z), \quad \mathbf{b} = (b_x, b_y, b_z), \quad \mathbf{c} = (c_x, c_y, c_z)$$

**Step 1 — Compute the inner cross product:**

$$\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c} = (b_y c_z - b_z c_y,\;\; b_z c_x - b_x c_z,\;\; b_x c_y - b_y c_x)$$

*Already ugly. Brace yourself.*

**Step 2 — Cross that with a, and isolate just the x-component:**

$$x\text{-component} = a_y(b_x c_y - b_y c_x) - a_z(b_z c_x - b_x c_z)$$

Expand it:

$$= a_y b_x c_y - a_y b_y c_x - a_z b_z c_x + a_z b_x c_z$$

Four terms, no obvious structure. And this is exactly the moment where the derivation does something *clever.*

---

### ✨ The Add-and-Subtract Trick

> *"The key that unlocks the door is adding zero in disguise."*

Add and subtract the exact same term — $a_x b_x c_x$ — to the expression. The value doesn't change. But the structure *does*:

$$b_x(a_x c_x + a_y c_y + a_z c_z) - c_x(a_x b_x + a_y b_y + a_z b_z)$$

Look at what appeared inside those parentheses:

- $(a_x c_x + a_y c_y + a_z c_z)$ — that's the **dot product $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}$**
- $(a_x b_x + a_y b_y + a_z b_z)$ — that's the **dot product $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$**

The x-component of the whole triple product is now:

$$b_x(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - c_x(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$$

Run the same logic for y and z — the pattern holds identically:

| Component | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| x-component | $b_x(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - c_x(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$ |
| y-component | $b_y(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - c_y(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$ |
| z-component | $b_z(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - c_z(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$ |

Now factor out the common dot products:

$$(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c})(b_x\hat{i} + b_y\hat{j} + b_z\hat{k}) - (\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})(c_x\hat{i} + c_y\hat{j} + c_z\hat{k})$$

Those bracketed expressions are just $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$. So:

$$\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$$

*Done. The monster reduces to a sentence.*

---

## 💡 The Geometric Meaning of the Scalars

The algebra confirmed the formula. But *why* are the scalars specifically dot products? There's a more satisfying answer.

Dot products measure **projection** — how much one vector aligns with another.

- $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}$ tells you how much of $\mathbf{a}$ points *along* $\mathbf{c}$
- $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$ tells you how much of $\mathbf{a}$ points *along* $\mathbf{b}$

So the formula is saying:

> *Scale $\mathbf{b}$ by how much $\mathbf{a}$ aligns with $\mathbf{c}$. Scale $\mathbf{c}$ by how much $\mathbf{a}$ aligns with $\mathbf{b}$. Subtract.*

The result lives in the plane of **b** and **c**, and the amount contributed by each vector depends on how strongly $\mathbf{a}$ leans toward the other. There's a beautiful symmetry here — **b gets the dot product involving c, and c gets the dot product involving b.** They borrow from each other.

---

## ❓ But Wait — Why the Minus Sign?

> *"The minus sign is not decorative. It is doing actual work."*

This is where many people nod and move on, when they should stop and check. Here are two sanity checks that make the minus sign obvious:

**Check 1 — When $\mathbf{a}$ is perpendicular to both $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$:**

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0, \quad \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c} = 0$$

So the right side becomes $\mathbf{b}(0) - \mathbf{c}(0) = \mathbf{0}$. Makes perfect sense — a vector perpendicular to both **b** and **c** is parallel to $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$, and $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}$.

**Check 2 — When $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{c}$:**

Then $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$, so the left side is $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$.

On the right side:

$$\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}) - \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{0} \checkmark$$

Without the minus sign, both checks would fail. The minus sign is the geometry encoded in algebra.

---

## 🌀 The Deepest Realization

Here is the moment that made a student in this very conversation stop typing and say *"oh my god"*:

The formula $\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$ is of the form:

$$x\mathbf{b} + y\mathbf{c}$$

And **by definition**, every vector of that form lies in the plane spanned by $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$.

No perpendicular arguments needed. No right-hand rule needed. No separate geometric proof needed. **The algebra already contains the geometry.** Once you write the answer as a linear combination of $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$, you've simultaneously proven it lives in their plane.

> *"At first the expression looks terrifying. But the identity says: Nope. It collapses entirely into the plane of b and c."*

This is what mathematicians mean when they call something elegant. The complexity folds in on itself and disappears.

---

## 🧠 How to Remember It Without Forgetting

For $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$:

1. **Take the first vector inside the parentheses** — that's $\mathbf{b}$
2. **Multiply it by the dot product of the outside vector with the second inside vector** — that's $(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c})$
3. **Subtract the second vector inside** — that's $\mathbf{c}$
4. **Multiplied by the dot product of the outside vector with the first inside vector** — that's $(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$

The mnemonic some people use: **BAC – CAB**

$$\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{C}) - \mathbf{C}(\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{B})$$

Say it like a word: *"back-cab."* It is a slightly absurd trick, but it works under exam pressure.

---

## ✅ Final Takeaway

The vector triple product identity isn't just a shortcut — it's a lens.

It reveals that **a nested cross product is not some new alien object**. It is simply a vector that lives in the plane of the two inner vectors, stretched and oriented by how much the outer vector leans toward each of them. The dot products are not there to make the formula look simple — they are there because *projection is the right language* for describing that leaning.

The derivation teaches you the add-and-subtract trick. The geometry teaches you *why the answer has to be a linear combination*. And the moment both of those thoughts land at the same time — when you realize the algebra and the geometry are saying the same thing from different directions — is the moment you stop memorizing formulas and start *seeing* them.

> *"Nested cross products, orientation complexity, and 3D geometry — all of it collapses into a simple planar linear combination."*
>
> **That is not a coincidence. That is the whole point.**

---

*The right-hand rule gave vectors their handedness. Lagrange's formula gave that handedness somewhere beautiful to land.*
